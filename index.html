The Opportunity of Symbiotic Relationships in Expanding Nutritional Security

By Robin Emmons

As the communications manager at a local nonprofit focused on food, something I regularly say to funders is “In an agricultural state like Kentucky, no one should be hungry.” And it is true, right? Yet, far too often areas of agricultural prosperity overlap with areas of high food insecurity. The two are by no means always related. I created this map to highlight the opportunity for cases when they are overlapping.

If we change the ways we address food insecurity, we can build better food systems for everyone. First, we need to change our goal from providing food security to nutritional security where people are getting the nutrients they need, not a temporary full belly feeling created by over processed carbs on repeat. Nutritional security means making fresh food available. The best place to look for fresh food? Local farms!

We can improve nutritional access while uplifting the local economy by purchasing local food. (And, let’s not forget that it is not uncommon for farmers to experience food insecurity as well depending on what there crops are.) We can create local organizations that are grounded in the immediate community and are able to alleviate hunger, help local farms, offer better health benefits, and are more sustainable by increasing access to fresh produce grown nearby. They need the purchasing power to do so. In buying local, there is no dicotomy of helper and the helped, it is a symbiotic relationship of helping each other.

At FoodChain, we have found buying local seconds and surplus to be more affordable and to help farmers profit from crops that often would have no market to enter. The crops are just as tasty, they just don’t look like what people expect in grocery stores.

Finding places where local food could alleviate local need is just one of the pieces to a puzzle that also involves preserving infrastructure and food or cooking education among other pieces. This map is to draw attention to places where meeting local need with local food could be especially game-changing. However, further research on specific locations to ensure it would be an appropriate solution that can flourish. Local food is not a one-size-fits-all solution. But it sure does have a lot of potential.

Map details

CRS: ESPG 102003 Albers Equal Area Conic

SNAP Data From: NHGIS IPUMS (SNAP Or Public Assistance by Households Data)
Farm Data From: ESRI Land Cover(Crops)
Caveats: using SNAP as the criteria to establish food insecurity is going to be an undercount. The "crops" land cover is defined by ESRI as: "Human planted/plotted cereals, grasses, and crops not at tree height; examples: corn, wheat, soy, fallow plots of structured land." Does not include food sources like orchards, but would include things like soy that alone are not sustaining or relevant to the kind of fresh produce purchasing discussed. This is why additional research into an area is needed and the map is just a starting point.
